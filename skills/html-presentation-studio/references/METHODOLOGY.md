# Methodology: from information to a presentation

## 1. Define the communication job

Write five lines before writing slides:

1. **Audience:** who is present and what do they already believe?
2. **Setting:** stage, meeting, async link, classroom, demo, or PDF?
3. **Action:** what should they decide, understand, try, or remember?
4. **One takeaway:** one complete sentence, not a topic label.
5. **Constraint:** duration, slide limit, brand, language, confidentiality, and evidence deadline.

A useful takeaway has a subject, claim, and consequence:

> Our release process is slow because verification is serialized, so parallel validation is the highest-return change.

## 2. Separate evidence from interpretation

Create a source ledger. Distinguish:

- observed fact;
- calculated result;
- cited external claim;
- interpretation;
- recommendation;
- assumption.

The deck may contain all six, but assumptions must not look like measured facts.

## 3. Choose a narrative architecture

### Executive decision

1. Decision required
2. Why now
3. Evidence
4. Constraints
5. Alternatives
6. Recommendation
7. Risks and mitigation
8. Owner and next step

### Product or company pitch

1. The world changed
2. The current workaround fails
3. The non-obvious insight
4. The product mechanism
5. Proof
6. Adoption or market path
7. Business model
8. Team and advantage
9. Ask

### Technical talk

1. Symptom
2. Constraints
3. Naive attempt
4. Why it failed
5. Architecture
6. Critical implementation detail
7. Demo or result
8. Trade-offs
9. Reusable lesson

### Teaching

1. Generative question
2. Mental model
3. Simple example
4. Boundary case
5. Common misconception
6. Guided practice
7. Independent check
8. Synthesis

### Research

1. Question
2. Why existing knowledge is insufficient
3. Method
4. Main result
5. Robustness/alternative explanation
6. Interpretation
7. Limits
8. Implication

## 4. Write assertion titles

Weak labels:

- Market
- Architecture
- Results
- Our solution
- Next steps

Stronger assertions:

- The addressable segment is concentrated in three regulated workflows
- A queue separates user latency from model latency
- Parallel checks cut median release time by 41%
- The product removes reconciliation rather than merely automating it
- A two-week pilot can test the only material adoption risk

Read titles in sequence. They should tell the story without the bodies.

## 5. Match visual form to reasoning

| Reasoning task | Visual form |
|---|---|
| magnitude | large metric with baseline and time period |
| trend | line/area chart with annotated inflection |
| composition | bar, stacked bar, or proportional blocks |
| process | directed flow with clear start and end |
| hierarchy | tree or nested structure |
| comparison | aligned table or common-scale small multiples |
| chronology | timeline |
| geography | map only when location is analytically relevant |
| mechanism | labeled diagram or staged animation |
| uncertainty | range, interval, scenario band, or confidence label |

Do not use diagrams merely to decorate prose.

## 6. Control pace

Alternate:

- dense evidence and quiet synthesis;
- overview and detail;
- problem and resolution;
- static explanation and demonstration;
- wide context and one precise number.

Use dividers only when the question changes.

## 7. Edit for speech

- prefer concrete nouns and active verbs;
- remove phrases such as “It is important to note that”;
- use parallel grammar in lists;
- keep labels short and explanation in notes;
- define jargon before abbreviation;
- move essential caveats onto the slide;
- keep deep methodology in notes or appendix.

## 8. Close with meaning

Avoid ending only with “Thank you” or “Questions?”. End on:

- the requested decision;
- the one takeaway;
- the action and owner;
- the generalizable principle;
- the unresolved question worth discussing.

Q&A may be secondary, not the main message.
